The 3R Framework

RECOGNIZE: The connections between hunger, stress, and health risks

REDUCE: Stigma around seeking help for any of these issues

RESOURCE: Connect peers to free, confidential services

Your Integrated Messages

Mental Health Messaging Practice 25 mins

  • Learn youth-friendly language
  • Practice supportive responses
  • Role-play scenarios
+

Key Principles

  • Normalize: Mental health struggles are common and valid
  • Validate: Acknowledge feelings without judgment
  • Connect: Link to resources without pressure
  • Follow-up: Check in shows you care
✅ DO Say:
  • "It's okay to not be okay"
  • "Your feelings are valid"
  • "Help is available, no questions asked"
  • "You deserve support"
  • "Mental health is health"
  • "Taking care of yourself is brave"
❌ DON'T Say:
  • "Just think positive"
  • "Others have it worse"
  • "You're being dramatic"
  • "Snap out of it"
  • "You're too young to be stressed"
  • Clinical terms without explanation

Scenario Practice

Scenario 1: A classmate mentions they've been too stressed to eat properly.

Your response framework:

  1. Validate: "That sounds really tough."
  2. Connect: "Stress and eating are definitely connected."
  3. Resource: "There's free food and support available if you want info."

Scenario 2: A friend says they're embarrassed to use the food pantry.

Practice responses that:

  • Normalize the experience
  • Share (if comfortable) that many students use resources
  • Emphasize confidentiality and dignity

Group Discussion

Health & Wellness Conversations for All Communities 20 mins

  • Culturally responsive messaging
  • Emphasize privacy and respect
  • Connect wellness to overall success
+

Universal Health Messages

  • "Your wellness matters" - Everyone deserves to be healthy
  • "Private and respectful care" - Services that honor your values
  • "Health is for everyone" - Inclusive services for all backgrounds
  • "Take care of your whole self" - Physical, mental, and emotional health

School-Friendly Talking Points

  • Connect health to academic success: "It's hard to focus when you're stressed or hungry"
  • Emphasize prevention: "Taking care of yourself now helps you reach your goals"
  • Respect family values: "These resources work with what's important to you and your family"
  • Focus on empowerment: "You have the right to take care of your health"

Culturally Responsive Practice

Key Approaches:

  • For concerns about family privacy: "Everything is confidential - your family's business stays private"
  • For language barriers: "Services are available in Spanish and other languages"
  • For cultural concerns: "Staff understand and respect different backgrounds"
  • For trust issues: "These are community services designed BY and FOR people like us"

Practice Activity: Role-play introducing health resources to:

  1. A student who might worry about family reputation
  2. Someone whose first language isn't English
  3. A peer who doesn't trust "the system"

Reflection

How can we make health conversations comfortable for students from all backgrounds? What words or approaches work best in your community?

Creative Peer Engagement Strategies 30 mins

  • Interactive engagement ideas
  • Quick activities for busy students
  • Making resources memorable
+

Beyond Handouts: Creative Engagement

1. "Stress Check" Stickers (2-minute interaction)

  • Create mood stickers (happy face, stressed face, hungry face)
  • Ask: "How's your day going?" and offer appropriate sticker
  • Each sticker has a resource on the back
  • Makes resources feel like peer support, not charity

2. "Myth Buster" Challenge (5-minute activity)

  • Quick true/false cards about health resources
  • Example: "True or False: You need parent permission for mental health support"
  • Correct answers earn resource cards
  • Makes learning interactive and fun

3. "Wellness Playlist" Exchange

  • Trade song recommendations for self-care
  • Each playlist card includes a wellness tip and resource
  • Connects to youth culture naturally

4. "Lunch Line Conversations"

  • Position near food areas during peak times
  • Natural opener: "Long line today, right? Hey, did you know about the free food programs?"
  • Non-threatening, casual approach

Quick Interaction Scripts

For shy students: Leave resources in visible spots with a note: "Take one if you need it - no questions asked"

For skeptical peers: "I'm just sharing what I learned - thought it might help someone"

For busy students: "Real quick - here's a card with free resources. Text the number if you ever need it"
